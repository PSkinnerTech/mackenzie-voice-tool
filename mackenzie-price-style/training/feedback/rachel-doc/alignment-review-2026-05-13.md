## Important caveat

This document is explicitly a Rachel Goodlad voice guide, not a MacKenzie Price guide. It must never be used as a target voice. Use it only as a reviewer/evaluation lens for Alpha parent communications: anti-marketing, no em dashes, concrete parent usefulness, and clarity. The target voice remains MacKenzie Price only, grounded in the MacKenzie Tier A corpus and MacKenzie review edits.

## Strong alignments with prior feedback

- No em dashes. The guide says: "No em dashes. Ever."
- Parent-to-parent, not seller-to-lead.
- Avoid marketing voice, sales-bro phrasing, hype, manufactured urgency, and generic warmth.
- Build know, like, trust.
- Substance first, CTA second.
- Shorter than instinct.
- Concrete examples over abstraction.
- Warm but not gushy.

## Where the Miami drafts were misaligned

- The initial draft sounded partly like brand/launch copy rather than a parent-insider note.
- Phrases like "that matters" and "energy this moment deserves" fail the guide's anti-generic-warmth test.
- The blog especially leaned into abstract launch/community language. The guide says outcomes over theory and storytelling over abstraction.
- The drafts did not include enough Head-of-Expansion substance: what the meetup actually accomplishes, what parents can expect, how it reduces first-day friction.
- The email was close after revision, but still can be more specific and less ceremonial.

## Calibration rules to add

1. Default posture: parent-to-parent trusted insider.
2. Keep first sentence direct. Avoid generic pleasantries.
3. Every paragraph should either make the parent feel known, liked, or able to trust the sender.
4. Avoid generic hype words: excited, thrilled, launch, journey, special, amazing, energy.
5. Use concrete first-day details: kids meeting each other, parents meeting guides/team, reducing first-day nerves, knowing where to go, seeing the space, asking practical questions.
6. No raw URLs. If links are used, embed as text.
7. No em dashes.
8. Avoid too much "founding family" reverence. Acknowledge it, but don't over-mythologize it.
9. Never draft in Rachel voice for this project. Rachel examples are evaluator context only.
10. For MacKenzie-authored pieces, keep MacKenzie parent/founder voice and borrow only the anti-marketing constraints.
